### Module 10: Post-Spin-off Employment & IP Waiver Framework

**1. Executive Summary & Strategic Directive for Project Phoenix**

The successful execution of Project Phoenix—a corporate spin-off strategy designed to incubate and eventually separate a high-growth technology venture from its legacy parent organization in Hong Kong—relies fundamentally on the watertight execution of employment and intellectual property (IP) transition mechanisms. At the heart of this transition is the co-founder, whose ongoing relationship with the legacy parent must be carefully managed to prevent the "contamination" of the spin-off's IP assets. 

This module provides an exhaustive framework for the Post-Spin-off Employment & IP Waiver Strategy. The central objective is to construct robust "Safe Harbor" clauses within the co-founder’s current employment contract and transition agreements. These clauses must achieve two critical ends: first, they must unconditionally release the parent organization's statutory and contractual claims over any IP related to the spin-off (specifically "peripheral" or moonlighting products); and second, they must establish legally enforceable boundaries that prevent the parent from invoking breach of fiduciary duty or restrictive covenants against the co-founder. 

To achieve this, our analysis deeply interrogates the Hong Kong Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57), particularly Section 31K concerning the continuity of employment across associated entities; the Patents Ordinance (Cap. 514), specifically Section 57 as it applies to the employer-employee IP ownership nexus; and the precise mechanics of restrictive covenants, focusing on the Hong Kong courts' adoption of the 6-month gold standard for non-compete reasonableness. Furthermore, we dissect pivotal local jurisprudence, namely *Acron International Technology Ltd v Chan Yiu Wai* [2017] 3 HKLRD 799 and *Cosme De Net Co Ltd v Lam Kin Ming* [2021] HKDC 445, to highlight the severe risks of undocumented "moonlighting" and the misappropriation of corporate resources. The module concludes with a granular Consent Waiver template engineered to immunize the co-founder from these localized legal threats.

**2. The Statutory Default for IP Ownership: Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance (Cap. 514)**

Under Hong Kong law, the default position regarding the ownership of intellectual property created by an employee is heavily skewed in favor of the employer. This principle is codified and strictly applied in the context of patentable inventions through the framework recognized under Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance (Cap. 514). While standard patent durations are broadly governed within this legislative context, the critical application in employment disputes focuses on the exact circumstances under which an invention is conceptualized and reduced to practice.

For the Project Phoenix co-founder, understanding this statutory default is a matter of existential importance for the spin-off. The law stipulates that an invention (and by extension, the patent rights to that invention) belongs to the employer if it satisfies one of two primary tests:

*   **The "Normal Duties" Test:** The invention belongs to the employer if it was made in the course of the employee's "normal duties" or specifically assigned duties, and it is an invention that might "reasonably be expected to result" from the carrying out of those duties. In the context of a co-founder who likely holds a senior technical or executive role within the parent company, their "normal duties" are typically construed very broadly by Hong Kong courts. If the co-founder is tasked with software architecture, product development, or technological innovation for the parent, any peripheral product they develop—even on weekends or utilizing personal hardware—runs a severe risk of being captured by this provision. The parent company can argue that the peripheral product is a natural extension of the co-founder's mandate to innovate, thereby triggering an automatic statutory assignment of the IP to the parent.
*   **The "Special Obligations" Test:** Even if an invention falls outside the strict boundaries of an employee's "normal duties," the IP will still vest in the employer if the employee has a "special obligation" to further the interests of the employer's undertaking. This is particularly perilous for directors, senior managers, and C-suite executives. Because these individuals owe elevated fiduciary duties of loyalty and fidelity to the company, the courts presume that any valuable innovation they create should belong to the entity they are duty-bound to advance. For the co-founder of Project Phoenix, maintaining dual status as a senior employee of the parent while incubating the spin-off triggers this special obligation test immediately.

Without an explicit, meticulously drafted Consent Waiver that specifically carves out the spin-off's technology from the definitions of "normal duties" and "special obligations," the parent company retains a lethal latent claim over the spin-off's core assets. This statutory default cannot be bypassed by mere verbal agreements or informal understandings; it requires a surgical legal instrument to disarm.

**3. Continuity of Employment and Associated Entities: Section 31K of the Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57)**

As Project Phoenix matures, the co-founder may be formally transferred from the legacy parent company payroll to the newly incorporated spin-off entity. This structural transition brings Section 31K of the Hong Kong Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57) into sharp focus. Section 31K governs the continuity of employment when an employee is transferred between "associated companies."

The legislation defines companies as "associated" if one is a subsidiary of the other, or if both are subsidiaries of a third corporate entity. The primary function of Section 31K is employee protection: it ensures that an employee's continuous period of service is not "broken" simply because they are moved across different nodes of a corporate group. 

*   **Severance and Long Service Liabilities:** Under Section 31K, the transfer of the co-founder from the parent to the spin-off (assuming the parent retains a majority stake, thereby making them associated) does not constitute a dismissal. Therefore, statutory severance payments or long service payments are not triggered at the time of the transfer. The co-founder's years of service at the parent are legally bridged and carried over to the spin-off. 
*   **Strategic Implications for Project Phoenix:** While Section 31K protects the employee's benefits, it also implies a continuity of the *employment relationship's obligations* unless explicitly severed. If the employment is deemed legally continuous across associated entities, the parent company might argue that the restrictive covenants and IP assignment clauses from the original employment contract remain in force or are implicitly inherited by the associated spin-off. 
*   **The Severance Tactic:** To achieve a clean break, the legal strategy must deliberately fracture the Section 31K continuity. The Consent Waiver and transition documents must explicitly state that the transfer is a mutual termination of the legacy employment contract, settling all accrued statutory entitlements (or explicitly waiving them in exchange for equity in the spin-off), and executing a completely new, unencumbered employment contract with the spin-off entity. Relying on Section 31K's automatic continuity is a trap that keeps the co-founder tethered to the parent company's original, restrictive terms.

**4. Restrictive Covenants: The 6-Month Gold Standard for Non-Competes in Hong Kong**

In drafting the transition framework, we must address the legacy non-compete clauses embedded in the co-founder's original employment contract with the parent. Hong Kong courts approach restrictive covenants with strict scrutiny. The starting position in common law is that all covenants in restraint of trade are void and unenforceable because they violate public policy favoring free commerce and employee mobility. 

However, a non-compete clause can be saved and enforced if the employer can prove that it is designed to protect a "legitimate business interest" (such as trade secrets, confidential information, or highly specialized customer connections) and that the scope of the restriction is "reasonable" in terms of duration, geographical area, and the nature of the restricted activities.

*   **The 6-Month Gold Standard:** Through evolving jurisprudence, Hong Kong courts have established a strong baseline regarding temporal reasonableness. While high-level executives might occasionally be held to 12-month restrictions if they possess "keys to the kingdom" regarding deeply sensitive proprietary algorithms or exclusive client lists, the **6-month period** is widely recognized as the gold standard for enforceability. Courts are highly reluctant to enforce non-competes extending beyond 6 months, viewing them as punitive rather than protective. If a parent company attempts to enforce a 12- or 24-month non-compete against the co-founder, the court is highly likely to strike it down entirely.
*   **Blue-Pencil Severance:** Hong Kong courts operate on a strict "blue-pencil" rule. They will not rewrite a poorly drafted, overly broad non-compete to make it reasonable. They will simply cross out the offending words. If crossing out the words leaves the clause grammatically nonsensical or alters its fundamental nature, the entire clause fails. 
*   **Waiver Integration:** The Consent Waiver must expressly supersede the parent's legacy non-compete. Even if the legacy non-compete is a reasonable 6-month restriction, the co-founder cannot operate Project Phoenix if it competes even tangentially with the parent. The Safe Harbor clause must explicitly waive the non-compete strictly in relation to the specific technologies and business models defined under the Project Phoenix business plan, rendering the 6-month restriction moot for this specific endeavor.

**5. Moonlighting and IP Misappropriation: Case Precedents in Hong Kong**

To comprehend the severity of the risks Project Phoenix faces without a Consent Waiver, we must analyze how Hong Kong courts ruthlessly penalize undocumented "moonlighting" (running a side business) and IP conflicts.

**5.1. Acron International Technology Ltd v Chan Yiu Wai [2017] 3 HKLRD 799**

The *Acron* case represents the definitive modern application of the statutory IP ownership framework against a "moonlighting" employee. 

*   **Facts:** Mr. Chan was employed by Acron International Technology specifically for his technical expertise in product development. During his employment, Chan developed a highly valuable patentable invention. Chan argued that he developed the invention on his own time, using his own resources, and that it was a peripheral concept separate from his daily tasks at Acron. He sought to retain ownership of the patent.
*   **Legal Issue:** Did the invention belong to the employer under the "normal duties" or "special obligations" tests, despite the employee's claims of independent development?
*   **Judgment:** The Court of Appeal ruled decisively in favor of the employer, Acron. The court ordered Chan to assign the patent entirely to the company.
*   **Rationale:** The court scrutinized Chan's employment contract and his actual role. Because he was hired to innovate and develop products in the technological domain relevant to Acron's business, the invention fell squarely within his "normal duties." The court emphasized that an invention "reasonably expected to result" from those duties belongs to the employer. The fact that Chan might have worked on it at home did not shield him. The court did order Acron to reimburse Chan for the out-of-pocket expenses he incurred in filing the patent, but the highly lucrative IP rights were stripped from him. (Source: [https://www.hklii.hk/en/cases/hkca/2017/454])
*   **Application to Project Phoenix:** *Acron* is a terrifying precedent for the co-founder. If the Project Phoenix technology is even tangentially related to the parent company's industry, the parent can invoke *Acron* to claim 100% ownership of the spin-off's IP. The parent will argue that the co-founder's "normal duties" as a senior executive included identifying new technological avenues, thus capturing the spin-off's core tech. A Consent Waiver is the only shield against the *Acron* precedent.

**5.2. Cosme De Net Co Ltd v Lam Kin Ming [2021] HKDC 445**

While *Acron* deals with IP ownership, the *Cosme De Net* case highlights the devastating consequences of secret moonlighting, focusing on summary dismissal and breach of fiduciary duty.

*   **Facts:** Lam Kin Ming was a Senior Business Development Manager for Cosme De Net, an online cosmetics retailer. Lam secretly established and operated a competing online store ("Best Online Cosme"). He sold identical products at lower margins and blatantly utilized Cosme De Net's proprietary product images and descriptions (IP infringement). He also funneled company inventory to his secret business. Upon discovery, he was summarily dismissed without notice or severance.
*   **Legal Issue:** Was the employer justified in utilizing summary dismissal under Section 9 of the Employment Ordinance for Lam's moonlighting activities?
*   **Judgment:** The District Court of Hong Kong upheld the summary dismissal, ruling that Lam's conduct constituted a fundamental and egregious breach of his employment contract and fiduciary duties.
*   **Rationale:** The court held that Lam, as a senior manager, owed a strict duty of good faith, fidelity, and loyalty. Operating a secret, competing business directly violated this duty. Furthermore, his unauthorized use of the employer's IP (the photographs) demonstrated dishonesty and misconduct inconsistent with the faithful discharge of his duties. The court granted an injunction to stop the IP infringement, though it struggled to award specific damages due to difficulties in proving direct financial loss linked solely to Lam's actions. (Source: [https://www.hklii.hk/en/cases/hkdc/2021/445])
*   **Application to Project Phoenix:** If the co-founder incubates Project Phoenix in secret without a Consent Waiver, they are replicating Lam's exact behavior in the eyes of the law. The parent company could summarily dismiss the co-founder, stripping them of all unvested legacy equity, bonuses, and severance. Furthermore, if the co-founder uses any parent company resources (servers, code snippets, even conceptual frameworks discussed during parent company time), the parent will seek immediate injunctions to shut down Project Phoenix, citing the fiduciary breaches established in *Cosme De Net*.

**6. Strategic Drafting: Protecting "Peripheral" Products via the Consent Waiver**

The primary challenge in drafting the Consent Waiver is utilizing specific wording that unequivocally prevents the parent company from claiming ownership of the spin-off's technology, which we will classify as "Peripheral Products" during the incubation phase. 

To achieve this, the wording must dismantle the statutory presumptions of Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance and neutralize the fiduciary duties highlighted in *Cosme De Net*. 

The wording must incorporate three strategic pillars:
1.  **Clear Delineation (The "Scope Carve-Out"):** The waiver must explicitly define the parent company's current business scope and formally state that the "Peripheral Products" (defined meticulously in an attached schedule) fall entirely *outside* the co-founder's "normal duties" and "special obligations." This directly attacks the *Acron* precedent.
2.  **Explicit Renunciation of IP Rights:** The parent must state, using irrevocable and binding language, that it waives all statutory, common law, and contractual rights to the intellectual property associated with the Peripheral Products. 
3.  **Authorized Moonlighting (The "Fiduciary Safe Harbor"):** The waiver must expressly grant the co-founder permission to expend time and effort on the Peripheral Products outside of standard working hours, explicitly stating that this activity does not constitute a breach of the duty of fidelity, loyalty, or any non-compete clauses. This neutralizes the *Cosme De Net* precedent.

**7. "Safe Harbor" Consent Waiver Template for Co-Founder**

The following framework provides the exact legal language required to execute the strategy outlined above. This must be integrated into the co-founder's employment file via a formal Deed of Variation or a standalone Consent and Waiver Agreement.

**DEED OF CONSENT AND INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY WAIVER**

**THIS DEED** is made on [Date]

**BETWEEN:**
(1) **[LEGACY PARENT COMPANY NAME]**, a company incorporated in Hong Kong whose registered office is at [Address] (the "**Company**"); and
(2) **[CO-FOUNDER NAME]**, holder of Hong Kong Identity Card number [HKID] (the "**Employee**").

**BACKGROUND:**
(A) The Employee is currently employed by the Company in the capacity of [Job Title] pursuant to an employment contract dated [Date] (the "**Employment Contract**").
(B) The Employee intends to independently develop, design, and commercialize certain technologies, software, and business concepts entirely separate from the Company’s core operations, specifically defined herein as the "Permitted Peripheral Project."
(C) The Company wishes to expressly consent to the Employee’s involvement in the Permitted Peripheral Project and unequivocally waive any and all claims to the intellectual property generated therein, creating a safe harbor for the Employee’s independent entrepreneurial activities.

**IT IS AGREED AS FOLLOWS:**

**1. Definitions**
"**Company Core Business**" means [Insert highly specific, narrow definition of the parent company's exact business, e.g., "the provision of legacy B2B supply chain logistics software utilizing on-premise Oracle databases"].
"**Permitted Peripheral Project**" means the research, development, and commercialization of [Insert exact description of Project Phoenix, e.g., "a decentralized blockchain-based consumer identity verification protocol"], the technical specifications of which are further detailed in Schedule 1.
"**Project IP**" means all intellectual property rights, including but not limited to patents, copyrights, trade secrets, source code, algorithms, and trademarks, created, authored, or discovered by the Employee (whether solely or jointly) that relate directly or indirectly to the Permitted Peripheral Project.

**2. Acknowledgment of Normal Duties (The Acron Carve-Out)**
2.1 The Company formally acknowledges and agrees that the development of the Permitted Peripheral Project falls entirely outside the scope of the Employee’s "normal duties" and any "specifically assigned duties" under their Employment Contract.
2.2 The Company further acknowledges that the Project IP is not an invention or creation that might reasonably be expected to result from the carrying out of the Employee's duties for the Company.
2.3 The Company expressly agrees that the Employee owes no "special obligation" to further the interests of the Company regarding the technological domain of the Permitted Peripheral Project.

**3. Irrevocable Waiver and Assignment of Intellectual Property**
3.1 To the extent that any operation of law (including but not limited to Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance (Cap. 514) or equivalent common law principles) vests any right, title, or interest in the Project IP in the Company, the Company hereby irrevocably and unconditionally waives, releases, and assigns all such rights, title, and interest in the Project IP back to the Employee in perpetuity.
3.2 The Company confirms it shall have no claim to any financial benefit, equity, royalties, or ownership stake in the Permitted Peripheral Project or any corporate entity formed to operate it.

**4. Authorization of Independent Activities (The Cosme De Net Safe Harbor)**
4.1 The Company grants express, written consent for the Employee to engage in the Permitted Peripheral Project during their own personal time (outside of the Company's standard working hours of [e.g., 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM, Monday to Friday]).
4.2 The Company agrees that the Employee’s involvement in the Permitted Peripheral Project shall not be deemed a breach of any fiduciary duty, duty of fidelity, duty of loyalty, or any implied term of mutual trust and confidence under Hong Kong employment law.
4.3 The Company explicitly waives the application of Clause [Insert Clause Number] (Non-Competition) and Clause [Insert Clause Number] (Conflict of Interest) of the Employment Contract strictly as they relate to the Employee's execution of the Permitted Peripheral Project. The Company acknowledges that the Permitted Peripheral Project does not constitute a competing business entity to the Company Core Business.

**5. Resource Segregation Covenant**
5.1 The Employee covenants that they shall not utilize any Company resources, including but not limited to Company-issued laptops, servers, proprietary data, confidential information, or the time of other Company personnel, in the development of the Permitted Peripheral Project. 
5.2 The Company acknowledges that, provided the Employee adheres to Clause 5.1, the creation of the Project IP is entirely independent of the Company's undertaking.

**6. Severance and Continuity (Section 31K Acknowledgment)**
6.1 In the event the Employee is transferred to a corporate entity established to manage the Permitted Peripheral Project, the Company and the Employee agree that such transfer shall be structured as a mutual termination of the Employment Contract and shall not rely on the continuity provisions of Section 31K of the Employment Ordinance (Cap. 57), ensuring a complete legal separation of obligations.

**IN WITNESS WHEREOF** this document has been executed as a Deed and delivered on the date first stated above.

*(Signature Blocks for Parent Company Director and Co-Founder)*
