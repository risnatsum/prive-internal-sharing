### Module 7: Founder Control & Governance Structures

**Objective**: Evaluate the efficacy of dual-class shares or holding company structures in proving "effective control" to vetting panels (TSSSU, Cyberport, HKSTP).
**Deep Analyst Directive**: Examine HKEX precedents, Private Equity frameworks (VIMA, HKVCA), voting rights ratios, and board seat allocation. Answer if a dual-class share structure can "save" eligibility when the parent holds >50% economic interest.

---

#### 1. Executive Summary and Analytical Thesis

In the execution of **Project Phoenix**—a corporate spin-off strategy designed to establish a Hong Kong-based entity that leverages the parent corporation's infrastructure while aggressively securing local innovation grants—the primary architectural challenge lies in satisfying conflicting mandates. Hong Kong’s public funding bodies (TSSSU, Cyberport, and HKSTP) possess stringent eligibility criteria engineered specifically to prevent mature, well-resourced parent companies from exploiting schemes intended for grassroots, independent entrepreneurs. Conversely, the parent company logically demands structural and economic dominance commensurate with its capital and infrastructure injections. 

This intelligence module evaluates whether complex governance mechanisms, specifically **dual-class shares (Weighted Voting Rights or WVR)** and layered **holding company structures**, can thread this needle. The central operational question is: *Can a dual-class share structure \"save\" eligibility for public funds if the corporate parent holds greater than 50% economic interest?*

**Analytical Conclusion:** Yes, conditionally. Under the Hong Kong Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622), private companies enjoy immense contractual freedom to sever the link between economic interest and voting power. Consequently, a subsidiary can legally construct a dual-class share architecture granting the individual founding team (the \"Founders\") 51%+ of voting power and board control, despite the parent holding an overwhelming majority of the equity. This satisfies the literal \"absolute control\" clauses of both Cyberport and HKSTP. However, vetting panels assess not just the *letter* of the law, but the *spirit* of the startup. Success requires a sophisticated, multi-layered approach involving contractual ring-fencing (via HKVCA or VIMA-aligned Term Sheets) and explicit differentiation of R&D from the parent company. Furthermore, while this structure survives private-market and incubator scrutiny, it inevitably collides with HKEX Chapter 8A listing rules upon an eventual IPO, necessitating a planned structural sunset.

#### 2. Legal Foundation: The Hong Kong Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622) and Private Company Flexibility

To establish a spin-off that achieves both parent economic dominance and founder operational control, one must rely on the flexibility of Hong Kong's private company regulations.

**The Severance of Economics and Voting**
In Hong Kong, the regulatory environment for private companies differs drastically from that of public companies. Under Section 588(4) of the Hong Kong Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622), a private company's voting rights are dictated entirely by its Articles of Association (AoA) rather than a rigid statutory formula. (Source: [https://www.cr.gov.hk/]) (Source: [https://smu.edu.sg/]). 

There is no statutory cap on the voting ratio for private entities. This means Project Phoenix could authorize \"Class A\" shares for the operating founders carrying 10, 50, or even 100 votes per share, while the parent company holds \"Class B\" ordinary shares carrying 1 vote per share, or strictly non-voting dividend-yielding shares. (Source: [https://www.conventuslaw.com/]) (Source: [https://www.cr.gov.hk/]). 

**The HK Law Society Position**
The Hong Kong Law Society has historically championed the \"freedom of contract\" for private companies, advocating that founders of high-growth tech firms should be permitted to protect their long-term vision against short-term capital pressures. In multiple submissions to the HKEX, the Law Society endorsed the use of WVRs provided adequate safeguards exist. For private entities, this translates to a robust legal precedent supporting customized governance in the AoA, shielding the structure from arbitrary legal challenges of \"unfair prejudice\" assuming all parties enter the shareholders' agreement knowingly. (Source: [https://www.conventuslaw.com/]) (Source: [https://smu.edu.sg/]).

#### 3. Navigating Vetting Panels: Eligibility & \"Effective Control\"

The core friction in Project Phoenix is passing the vetting panels of Hong Kong's trio of premier funding schemes. Each scheme scrutinizes \"founder control\" slightly differently, and each presents a unique vulnerability to a heavy parent-company presence.

##### 3.1. Cyberport Incubation Programme (CIP)
Cyberport is notoriously strict regarding its mandate to support independent entrepreneurs. The foundational rule dictates that the founders must collectively hold **at least 51% of the company’s issued shares OR have absolute control** over the company. (Source: [https://www.cyberport.hk/]) (Source: [https://startupregistry.hk/]).

*   **The Subsidiary Risk:** If the parent company simply owns >50% of standard ordinary shares, the spin-off is automatically ineligible. A 100% corporate subsidiary is instantly disqualified. 
*   **The WVR Solution:** This is where the dual-class share structure \"saves\" the application. By leveraging the \"OR absolute control\" clause, Project Phoenix can allocate 80% of the economic equity to the parent company (Class B) and 20% to the individual founders (Class A). If Class A shares hold a 10:1 voting ratio, the founders possess approximately 92.5% of the total voting power. 
*   **Vetting Panel Reality:** While legally compliant with the \"absolute control\" clause, the Cyberport vetting panel (composed of venture capitalists, academics, and industry veterans) will aggressively scrutinize the *independence* of the project. If the parent company retains sweeping veto rights over daily operations or IP monetization, the panel may reject the application on the subjective grounds of lacking \"entrepreneurial risk.\" Thus, board seat allocation (discussed in Section 5) becomes paramount. (Source: [https://www.cyberport.hk/]) (Source: [https://theblockbeats.info/]).

##### 3.2. Hong Kong Science and Technology Parks (HKSTP) Incubation
HKSTP operates programs like Incu-Tech, Incu-App, and Incu-Bio. Their framework is slightly more accommodating to corporate subsidiaries, provided the technology is highly differentiated. 
*   **The Thresholds:** For standard incubation, founders must hold 51% shareholding or prove absolute control. Interestingly, for **Incu-Bio**, recognizing the massive capital requirements of biotech, HKSTP lowers the mandatory founder economic interest to **33%**, provided control is maintained. (Source: [https://www.hkstp.org/]) (Source: [https://www.aspireapp.com/]).
*   **Parent Company Differentiation:** HKSTP specifically forbids corporate shareholders (up to the ultimate holding company) from being \"mature companies\" in the *same industry* engaged in the R&D of the *same or similar products*. (Source: [https://www.hkstp.org/]) (Source: [https://www.smelink.gov.hk/]).
*   **Saving Eligibility:** A WVR structure perfectly saves HKSTP eligibility by fulfilling the \"absolute control\" test even at a minority economic stake. However, Project Phoenix must explicitly document that the subsidiary's R&D IP is completely ring-fenced from the parent company to survive the \"mature competitor\" prohibition.

##### 3.3. Technology Start-up Support Scheme for Universities (TSSSU & TSSSU+)
TSSSU funding, routed through the six designated local universities (e.g., HKU, CUHK, HKUST), focuses heavily on the \"Person-in-Charge\" (PIC), who must be a university member.
*   **Holding Company Tolerance:** TSSSU is unique in explicitly allowing \"indirect control.\" Universities like CUHK define an \"effective majority\" as direct or indirect holding. This means a holding company structure (Parent $\rightarrow$ Holding Co $\rightarrow$ Spin-off) is permissible, provided the university PIC sits at the apex of control. (Source: [https://www.cuhk.edu.hk/]) (Source: [https://www.hku.hk/]).
*   **The TSSSU+ Vulnerability:** For the TSSSU+ matching grant, if the parent company acts as the \"private investor,\" it trips the \"mature company\" restriction. TSSSU rules state that corporate investors must not be mature companies in the same industry. While this can be waived if they are the direct matching investor, funds must flow directly into the applicant start-up. A complex WVR structure here might trigger bureaucratic delays as university Tech Transfer Offices (TTOs) are generally more accustomed to simple cap tables. (Source: [https://www.hku.hk/]) (Source: [https://www.cuhk.edu.hk/]).

#### 4. Private Equity Frameworks: VIMA and HKVCA

To structure the governance of Project Phoenix, relying solely on voting ratios is insufficient. Vetting panels will look at the underlying Shareholders' Agreement (SHA). We must analyze standard Private Equity frameworks—specifically the Venture Capital Investment Model Agreements (VIMA) and the Hong Kong Venture Capital and Private Equity Association (HKVCA) Model Term Sheets.

**Reserved Matters and Veto Rights**
In private equity, control is not just about affirmative voting; it is about negative control (vetoes). Both VIMA (which is Singapore-derived but heavily used in HK regional deals) and HKVCA define \"Reserved Matters\"—actions the company cannot take without the investor's (the parent's) consent. (Source: [https://www.mofo.com/]) (Source: [https://www.svca.org.sg/]).

*   **The Conflict:** If Project Phoenix utilizes a standard HKVCA term sheet, the parent company will likely demand vetoes over annual budgets, executive hiring, and business pivots. Cyberport and HKSTP panels will read this SHA and conclude that the founders do *not* have absolute control, nullifying the WVR structure's protection.
*   **The Solution:** The SHA must be meticulously tailored. The parent company's Reserved Matters must be restricted strictly to \"fundamental corporate actions\" (e.g., liquidation, IPO, issuance of new equity, sale of core IP) rather than operational metrics. Operational control, hiring, and day-to-day R&D must explicitly fall under the sole purview of the founder-led board to satisfy incubator panels. (Source: [https://www.mofo.com/]) (Source: [https://www.svca.org.sg/]).

**Board Seat Allocation Precedents**
Voting rights on shares only apply at General Meetings. Daily control happens at the Board of Directors.
To \"save\" eligibility, the founders must control the board. If the board has 3 seats, founders must hold 2, and the parent company holds 1. Even if the parent has 80% economic ownership, granting them board majority is an immediate red flag for incubators. The parent's protection must be codified via protective provisions (vetoes on fundamental actions), not numerical board superiority. (Source: [https://www.vglawfirm.com/]) (Source: [https://www.nvca.org/]).

#### 5. HKEX Precedents: The End-Game Collision

Project Phoenix must operate with the end-game in mind. If the ultimate goal is a spin-off IPO on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange (HKEX), the private WVR structure that successfully navigated incubator panels will face severe regulatory headwinds under Chapter 8A of the Listing Rules.

##### 5.1. Chapter 8A and the \"Individual Beneficiary\" Mandate
Introduced in 2018, Chapter 8A permits WVR structures for \"Innovative Companies\" with a minimum market cap of HK$10 billion (or HK$40 billion). However, a critical restriction exists: **WVR beneficiaries must be individuals** (specifically, directors materially responsible for the company's growth). (Source: [https://www.withersworldwide.com/]) (Source: [https://www.hkex.com.hk/]).

Corporate entities—such as the parent company in Project Phoenix—are strictly prohibited from holding WVR shares in a primary listing. The HKEX conducted a consultation in 2020 to potentially allow corporate WVRs, but ultimately rejected the proposal to protect minority public investors. (Source: [https://www.hkex.com.hk/]) (Source: [https://www.withersworldwide.com/]).

##### 5.2. The Alibaba and JD Health Precedents
Two major market precedents illustrate how this plays out for corporate spin-offs:
*   **Alibaba (9988.HK) - The Exception:** Alibaba successfully listed with a corporate-esque WVR structure (the Alibaba Partnership nomination rights). However, this was only permitted because Alibaba was a \"Grandfathered\" Greater China Issuer, utilizing a secondary listing pathway after already being listed in New York. This pathway is completely unavailable for a new, grassroots spin-off like Project Phoenix. (Source: [https://www.hkexnews.hk/]) (Source: [https://www.publicnow.com/]).
*   **JD Health (6618.HK) - The Rule:** When the tech giant JD.com (which itself has a WVR structure) spun off its subsidiary JD Health on the HKEX, it was forced to do so under a standard **one-share, one-vote** framework. JD.com could not hold WVR shares in JD Health. It maintained control simply through a massive majority of standard ordinary shares. (Source: [https://www.hkexnews.hk/]) (Source: [https://www.hkex.com.hk/]).

**Strategic Implication for Phoenix:** The dual-class structure with a 10:1 voting ratio that we implement today to appease Cyberport/HKSTP while protecting the parent's economics *cannot survive an HKEX IPO*. The parent company cannot be granted WVR shares. If the *founders* hold the WVR shares to maintain control against the parent and public markets, their voting power is strictly capped at **10:1** by HKEX rules, and they must hold at least 10% of the underlying economic interest. Furthermore, standard \"sunset clauses\" will apply—meaning if the founders leave, the WVR structure collapses.

#### 6. Strategic Architecture for Project Phoenix

To successfully navigate the private incubator phase and prepare for the public markets, Project Phoenix should adopt the following hybrid architecture:

1.  **Economic Disproportionation (The Cap Table):** The Parent Company injects capital for an 80% equity stake, issued as Class B Ordinary Shares. The Founders are issued a 20% equity stake as Class A WVR Shares.
2.  **The 10:1 Voting Ratio:** The Class A shares are granted a 10:1 voting ratio in the Articles of Association. This yields the founders ~71.4% of the total voting power, legally satisfying the \"absolute control\" requirements of Cyberport, HKSTP, and TSSSU. We cap this at 10:1 (rather than the unlimited ratio allowed by Cap. 622) to align smoothly with future HKEX Chapter 8A requirements, avoiding a jarring structural reset pre-IPO.
3.  **Board Sovereignty:** The Board consists of 3 members. The Founders appoint 2; the Parent appoints 1. This proves operational dominance to vetting panels.
4.  **VIMA-Aligned Ring-Fencing:** The Shareholders' Agreement borrows from VIMA frameworks, excising operational vetoes. The Parent retains \"Reserved Matters\" vetoes only over fundamental economic events (Sale, Liquidation, IPO, issuance of senior debt).
5.  **TSSSU Alignment:** The CEO/PIC is officially appointed from the university ecosystem, directly holding a portion of the Class A WVR shares to ensure TSSSU compliance regarding direct PIC control.
6.  **The IPO Sunset Protocol:** A legally binding clause in the SHA dictates that upon filing an A1 application to the HKEX, the Parent’s economic majority transitions to standard public shares, while the Founders' Class A shares undergo scrutiny to ensure compliance with the Chapter 8A \"Innovative Company\" individual beneficiary rules.
