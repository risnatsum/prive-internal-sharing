### Module 6: Shareholding Thresholds & Dependency Analysis

The successful execution of "Project Phoenix"—the corporate spin-off of a new Hong Kong entity—hinges on navigating the complex web of shareholding "Red Lines" defined by the Companies Registry, the Innovation and Technology Commission (ITC), Cyberport, and the Hong Kong Science and Technology Parks Corporation (HKSTP). For a spin-off to remain eligible for TSSSU, Cyberport Incubation, or HKSTP Incubation, it must maintain a delicate balance between leveraging parent infrastructure and demonstrating a level of independence that satisfies the "Significant Control" and "Mature Company" restrictions.

This module maps the exact equity thresholds and dependency triggers that determine whether a spin-off is viewed as a genuine independent startup or a mere corporate R&D department.

---

#### 1. The "Red Lines" of Equity: 25%, 30%, 49%, and 51%

The regulatory landscape in Hong Kong uses four critical percentage thresholds to define control and dependency.

*   **The 25% Threshold (Significant Control):** Under the *Companies Ordinance (Cap. 622)*, any entity holding **more than 25%** of a company's shares or voting rights is a "Significant Controller" and must be recorded in the Significant Controllers Register (SCR). For funding bodies like HKSTP, if a **\"Mature Company\"** (defined as a profitable, established corporation) holds >25%, the startup is frequently disqualified as it is seen as a \"controlled entity\" of a mature firm (Source: HKSTP Incubation Guidelines; Companies Registry SCR FAQ).
*   **The 30% Threshold (Related Entities):** The ITC uses a **30% ownership mark** to identify \"Related Entities.\" While rules were relaxed in 2020 to allow related entities to apply for separate TVP grants, this threshold remains the standard for mandatory disclosure of major shareholders in BUD Fund and ESS applications (Source: ITC TVP FAQ; BUD Fund Guide).
*   **The 49% Threshold (Parent Cap):** For Cyberport and HKSTP, the logical inverse of the \"51% Founder Rule\" is a **49% cap** on external or corporate parent ownership. If a parent company holds more than 49%, the founders lose majority equity control, triggering an automatic \"absolute control\" audit (Source: Cyberport Incubation Guide; HKSTP Incu-Tech Guide).
*   **The 51% Threshold (Founder Control):** This is the \"Golden Rule\" for Hong Kong incubation. The founding team must collectively hold **at least 51%** of the issued shares at the time of application and throughout the program. This ensures the founders, not the parent corporation, are \"in the driver's seat\" (Source: Cyberport CIP Notes; HKSTP Operations Manual).

---

#### 2. Entity-Specific Thresholds and Disqualifiers

Each funding body interprets \"dependency\" through its own lens, ranging from strict equity counts to nuanced \"Significant Influence\" tests.

##### A. TSSSU (ITC & Universities)
The TSSSU scheme is the most flexible regarding corporate spin-offs but the most rigid regarding university stakes.
*   **University Cap:** The associated university should generally not hold more than **20%** of the shares.
*   **Founder Stake:** Universities like CUHK require active members to hold an **\"effective majority\"** (typically >50%), while HKU requires at least **20%** and HKUST **10%**.
*   **Mature Company Rule:** Corporate shareholders cannot be \"mature companies\" in the same industry. However, for **TSSSU+**, this is waived if the parent is the private investor providing the 1:1 matching funds (Source: ITC TSSSU Application Guidelines 2024/25).

##### B. Cyberport Incubation Programme (CIP)
Cyberport focuses on \"Absolute Control\" as the primary metric for independence.
*   **The 51% Rule:** Founders must own 51%. If they own less (e.g., 30-49%), they must provide a **Shareholders' Agreement (SHA)** proving they hold \"Absolute Control\" through board appointment rights or veto powers over reserved matters.
*   **Dependency Audit:** If a parent company provides the majority of the startup's revenue or infrastructure, Cyberport may disqualify the entity as an \"internal project\" rather than a startup (Source: Cyberport CIP Eligibility FAQ).

##### C. HKSTP Incubation (Incu-Tech / Incu-Bio)
HKSTP uses a \"Differentiated R&D\" test for corporate subsidiaries.
*   **Incu-Tech:** Founding team must hold **$\ge 51\%$**. Corporate parent stake is capped at **49%**.
*   **Incu-Bio:** Threshold is lower, requiring only **$\ge 33\%$** founder equity, allowing a parent or VC to own up to **67%**.
*   **R&D Staff Equity:** At least **10%** of equity should ideally be held by full-time R&D staff (excluding founders) to ensure team stability (Source: HKSTP Incubation Programme Guide).

---

#### 3. Defining \"Significant Control\" & \"Mature Company\"

The disqualification of a spin-off often stems not from the *amount* of shares held by a parent, but the *type* of parent holding them.

*   **Significant Influence (The Invisible Red Line):** Even if a parent company holds only **10-15%** of the equity, it can be deemed to have \"Significant Control\" if it holds veto rights over:
    1.  Changes to the business plan.
    2.  Appointment/removal of the CEO.
    3.  Intellectual Property (IP) licensing terms.
    If the ITC or an incubator determines the parent exercises this level of influence, the startup is disqualified for lacking independence (Source: Companies Ordinance Cap. 622, Sch. 5A).
*   **The Mature Company Disqualifier:** A \"Mature Company\" is an entity that is already established and profitable in the same industry. If such a company is a major shareholder, the government views the startup as a subsidized R&D arm. To avoid this, Project Phoenix must prove the spin-off is targeting a **different market segment** or using **different core technology** than the parent (Source: HKSTP Guiding Principles for Corporate Shareholders).
