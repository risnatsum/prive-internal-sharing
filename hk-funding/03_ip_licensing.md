### Module 3: IP "Originality" & Licensing Architecture

**Objective:** Define the specific legal and strategic mechanisms, particularly within an Intellectual Property (IP) Licensing Agreement, required to definitively prove that "core R&D" takes place within the Hong Kong (HK) spin-off entity rather than its parent company. This module provides the architectural framework necessary to leverage parent company infrastructure while satisfying the stringent "originality" and independence criteria demanded by Hong Kong's primary funding and incubation bodies: the Technology Start-up Support Scheme for Universities (TSSSU), Cyberport, and the Hong Kong Science and Technology Parks Corporation (HKSTP).

#### 1. Strategic Imperative and Context (Project Phoenix)
For Project Phoenix, spinning out an entity in Hong Kong that maintains a lifeline to a parent company's resources—while simultaneously presenting itself as an independent, innovative startup—requires walking a precise legal tightrope. Hong Kong's innovation funding ecosystem is designed to subsidize and accelerate *new, local* intellectual property creation, not to subsidize the outsourced R&D departments of mature corporations. 

To secure funding and incubation space, the spin-off must irrevocably demonstrate that its core value proposition stems from novel, independent intellectual effort occurring within Hong Kong. If the parent company retains too much control over the newly created IP, or if the licensing agreement implies that the spin-off is merely a commercialization vehicle for the parent's ongoing R&D, applications to Cyberport, HKSTP, and TSSSU will be rejected under the grounds of lacking "originality" or violating "founder independence" mandates.

#### 2. Definitional Framework: WIPO and HK IPD Standards
To draft an airtight licensing agreement, we must adopt the standardized taxonomy recognized by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) and the Hong Kong Intellectual Property Department (IPD). Mischaracterizing these elements is the most common cause of failure in technology transfer audits.

**2.1 Background IP (BIP)**
According to WIPO guidelines, Background IP consists of all intellectual property, know-how, and trade secrets that a party owns, controls, or has developed *prior* to the commencement of the specific collaborative project or spin-off formation (Source: https://www.wipo.int/about-ip/en/universities_research/ip_policies/). In the context of Project Phoenix, this represents the parent company's existing infrastructure, software libraries, patented algorithms, and established trade secrets. The parent company retains absolute ownership of this BIP. Crucially, the Hong Kong IPD emphasizes that the transfer or sharing of BIP must be governed by strict confidentiality undertakings to prevent inadvertent forfeiture of trade secret protection under Hong Kong's common law of confidence (Source: https://www.ipd.gov.hk/en/resources/publications/leaflets-and-booklets/index.html).

**2.2 Foreground IP (FIP) / Project IP**
Foreground IP is defined by WIPO as any new intellectual property—including inventions, software code, designs, and know-how—created, conceived, or developed directly during the execution of the project or by the spin-off entity (Source: https://www.wipo.int/about-ip/en/universities_research/ip_policies/). For Project Phoenix, FIP is the "core R&D" that must be proven to originate in Hong Kong. Ownership of FIP is not determined by default statutory law in commercial collaborations but is entirely a matter of contractual negotiation (freedom of contract).

**2.3 Improvement IP (Severable vs. Non-Severable)**
Improvement IP bridges the gap between BIP and FIP. It refers to modifications, enhancements, or derivatives of the original Background IP. 
*   **Severable Improvements:** These are new inventions that can operate independently of the underlying BIP. In Hong Kong technology transfer practice, these are almost universally owned by the party that creates them (the spin-off) (Source: https://www.ipd.gov.hk/en/resources/publications/leaflets-and-booklets/index.html).
*   **Non-Severable Improvements:** These enhancements cannot function without infringing upon the underlying BIP. Parent companies typically attempt to claim ownership of non-severable improvements. However, to satisfy HKSTP and Cyberport criteria, the spin-off *must* retain ownership of these improvements, granting only a limited "grant-back" license to the parent.

#### 3. Navigating "Originality" Across HK Funding Schemes
Each major funding body in Hong Kong applies the concept of "originality" differently, utilizing it both as a legal standard for IP protection and a vetting criterion to screen out corporate subsidiaries.

**3.1 Cyberport Incubation Programme (CIP)**
Cyberport evaluates originality under its "Uniqueness, Market Niche, and Innovation" pillar. Cyberport is explicitly an equity-free and IP-free program; however, it requires the startup to own all IP rights necessary to execute its business plan (Source: https://www.cyberport.hk/en/entrepreneurship/cyberport_incubation_programme).
*   **The 51% Founder Rule:** To qualify as an independent startup rather than a subsidiary, the individual founders must collectively hold at least 51% of the spin-off's shares (Source: https://www.cyberport.hk/en/entrepreneurship/cyberport_incubation_programme). If the parent company holds a majority stake, the application will fail.
*   **Non-Infringement Warranty:** The spin-off must provide a legal warranty that its technology does not infringe on third-party IP, including its parent company's IP. This necessitates a formal, written IP assignment or licensing agreement from the parent prior to application.

**3.2 Hong Kong Science and Technology Parks (HKSTP)**
HKSTP evaluates projects under the "Innovativeness" criteria and strictly enforces a "Mature Company" rule. 
*   **Independence from Mature Parents:** If the parent company is deemed "mature," it cannot be engaged in the exact same R&D as the spin-off. The spin-off's R&D must be demonstrably distinct (Source: https://www.hkstp.org/).
*   **IP Position:** HKSTP requires a "clear IP position." If the spin-off relies on the parent's infrastructure, the parent must execute an Exclusive License Agreement or an outright IP Assignment for the specific field of use. A non-exclusive license is often viewed unfavorably as it implies the parent is still actively competing or directing the technology.

**3.3 Technology Start-up Support Scheme for Universities (TSSSU)**
TSSSU is designed to commercialize university research. The Innovation and Technology Commission (ITC) provides the funding but takes no IP or equity (Source: https://www.itf.gov.hk/). IP negotiations are handled directly with the university's Technology Transfer Office (TTO).
*   **Certificate of Originality:** Universities (e.g., HKU, CUHK) require applicants to sign a Declaration or Certificate of Originality, swearing that the R&D results are the team's own work and do not infringe on existing rights (Source: https://www.cuhk.edu.hk/).
*   **University IP vs. Start-up IP:** If the startup relies on "Background IP" developed using university resources, the university owns it, and the startup must negotiate a formal license. Conversely, "Foreground IP" generated *after* the spin-off is formed, using TSSSU funds and independent resources, belongs entirely to the startup. Maintaining a strict firewall between university lab usage and startup operations is essential to protect the originality and ownership of this Foreground IP.

#### 4. The Licensing Architecture: Satisfying Originality Requirements
To satisfy the requirements of Cyberport, HKSTP, and TSSSU while leveraging the parent's infrastructure, the IP Licensing Agreement between the parent and the spin-off must be architected around the concept of **"Field of Use" exclusivity** and **Foreground IP capture**.

**Mechanism 1: The "Field of Use" Exclusive License**
Instead of the parent assigning (selling) all its IP to the spin-off, it grants an *exclusive* license limited to a highly specific "Field of Use" (e.g., "AI-driven wealth management applications in the APAC region"). 
*   **Why it works:** This allows the parent to retain ownership of its core infrastructure (BIP) for other global operations, satisfying corporate needs. Simultaneously, because the license is *exclusive* within the spin-off's domain, Cyberport and HKSTP view the spin-off as having a monopoly over its niche, satisfying the "clear IP position" requirement.

**Mechanism 2: Asymmetric Improvement Ownership**
Standard corporate licenses often stipulate that any improvements made by the licensee belong to the licensor. **This clause is fatal to a Hong Kong incubation application.**
*   **Why it works:** The agreement must explicitly state that the spin-off (Licensee) owns all Foreground IP and Improvement IP generated in Hong Kong. The spin-off can grant the parent a non-exclusive, royalty-free "grant-back" license to use these improvements outside the exclusive Field of Use. This proves to vetting committees that the "core R&D" (the improvements and new applications) is genuinely taking place in Hong Kong and adding localized value.

**Mechanism 3: The "Firewall" R&D Clause**
To explicitly prove core R&D happens in HK, the agreement must legally mandate a firewall. It should state that the parent company will provide infrastructure access (APIs, documentation), but *all* algorithmic development, adaptation, and market-specific innovation must be executed by personnel employed directly by the HK spin-off, utilizing funding deposited in HK banking institutions.

#### 5. Required Clauses in the IP Licensing Agreement
The following specific clauses must be drafted into the parent/spin-off licensing agreement to survive legal due diligence by Hong Kong funding bodies.

**Clause 1: Definition and Retention of Background IP**
> "The Parties acknowledge that Parent Company is the sole and exclusive owner of the Parent Infrastructure and all associated Background IP. Nothing in this Agreement shall be construed as an assignment or transfer of ownership of any Background IP to the Spin-off Entity. Parent Company grants to the Spin-off Entity an exclusive, royalty-free license to utilize the Background IP solely within the defined Field of Use."
*   *Analyst Note:* Clearly demarcates pre-existing value from new value.

**Clause 2: Absolute Ownership of Foreground IP (Core R&D)**
> "All Foreground IP, including but not limited to software code, algorithms, hardware modifications, and business processes conceived, reduced to practice, or developed by the Spin-off Entity during the Term of this Agreement, shall be the sole and exclusive property of the Spin-off Entity. The Spin-off Entity warrants that all R&D activities leading to the creation of Foreground IP shall be conducted substantially within the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region by personnel directly employed by the Spin-off Entity."
*   *Analyst Note:* This is the definitive clause proving originality. It explicitly localizes the R&D effort to Hong Kong, satisfying the geographical intent of Cyberport/HKSTP/TSSSU funding.

**Clause 3: Improvement IP and Grant-Back Provision**
> "In the event that the Spin-off Entity creates any Severable or Non-Severable Improvements to the Parent Company’s Background IP, ownership of all right, title, and interest in such Improvement IP shall vest immediately and exclusively in the Spin-off Entity. The Spin-off Entity hereby grants to the Parent Company a non-exclusive, perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free license to use said Improvement IP strictly outside of the Spin-off Entity's exclusive Field of Use."
*   *Analyst Note:* Reverses standard corporate gravity. It ensures the startup accumulates asset value (IP) over time, which is necessary for future VC funding and validates the spin-off's independent valuation.

**Clause 4: Warranty of Originality and Non-Infringement**
> "The Parent Company warrants that, to the best of its knowledge, the Background IP licensed hereunder does not infringe upon the intellectual property rights of any third party. The Spin-off Entity warrants that all Foreground IP generated shall be its original creation and shall not incorporate third-party IP without express written authorization."
*   *Analyst Note:* Mirrors the language required in the TSSSU "Certificate of Originality."

**Clause 5: "Course of Employment" Waiver**
> "The Parent Company formally waives any rights, under Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance (Cap. 514) or Section 14 of the Copyright Ordinance (Cap. 528) of Hong Kong, to claim ownership over inventions, works, or intellectual property created by founders or personnel of the Spin-off Entity, notwithstanding any prior employment relationship between said personnel and the Parent Company."
*   *Analyst Note:* Crucial for spin-offs where founders were previously employed by the parent. It legally severs the parent's default statutory right to claim the new IP.

#### 6. Hong Kong Case Law Precedents
When drafting the licensing architecture, we must anticipate how Hong Kong courts interpret IP ownership disputes. The common law system heavily relies on precedent.

**Employment vs. Innovation: *Acron International Technology Ltd v Chan Yiu Wai* [2017] 3 HKLRD 799**
*   **Facts:** Former employees patented an invention shortly after leaving the plaintiff company. The invention was heavily based on their work during their employment (Source: https://www.hcamag.com/).
*   **Ruling:** The HK Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the employer, applying Section 57 of the Patents Ordinance. The court held that if the "intellectual effort" occurred during normal duties, the employer owns the resulting patent, even if filed post-employment.
*   **Strategic Application for Project Phoenix:** If Project Phoenix founders begin developing the spin-off technology while still employed by the parent, the parent *automatically* owns it under HK law. This necessitates the "Course of Employment Waiver" (Clause 5 above) to ensure the spin-off actually owns its starting IP.

**Trade Secrets and Independence: *Cosme De Net Co Ltd v Lam Kin Ming* [2021] HKDC 445**
*   **Facts:** An employee used proprietary data to set up a competing business (Source: https://www.mayerbrown.com/).
*   **Ruling:** The District Court upheld the employee's dismissal for breaching the common law duty of confidence.
*   **Strategic Application for Project Phoenix:** Because the parent company is sharing its infrastructure (trade secrets/know-how), the licensing agreement must explicitly carve out what confidential information the spin-off is permitted to use for its own commercial gain. Without this explicit carve-out, the spin-off operates under a constant threat of a breach of confidence lawsuit from its own parent.

**Licensing Boundaries: *Warner Bros. Entertainment Inc. v. 9¾ Cafe* [2023]**
*   **Facts:** A cafe used thematic elements without a license, arguing "inspiration."
*   **Ruling:** The High Court established strict liabilities for unauthorized use and substantial copying.
*   **Strategic Application for Project Phoenix:** Demonstrates that HK courts strictly enforce licensing boundaries. The "Field of Use" must be meticulously defined; if the spin-off strays outside its licensed field, it commits infringement, triggering default clauses that could revoke its IP rights entirely.

#### 7. Strategic Optimization: Tax and Policy Incentives
Properly structuring the IP to prove ownership in Hong Kong opens up significant financial advantages beyond just incubation funding.
*   **Patent Box Tax Incentive:** By demonstrating that the R&D and ownership reside in the HK spin-off, the entity becomes eligible for the "Patent Box" tax regime. Profits derived from qualifying IP (Foreground IP patents filed in HK) are taxed at a concessional rate of 5%, rather than the standard corporate rate of 16.5% (Source: https://www.fso.gov.hk/).
*   **Original Grant Patent (OGP) System:** The spin-off should utilize Hong Kong's OGP system (launched in 2019) to file patents directly in HK based on its Foreground IP. An OGP filing provides irrefutable, government-backed proof of "Originality" and local R&D effort to any vetting committee.
