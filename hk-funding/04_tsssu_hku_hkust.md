### Module 4: ITC TSSSU Framework: HKU vs. HKUST Implementation

#### 1. Executive Summary: The Divergent Philosophies of Tech Transfer
The Technology Start-up Support Scheme for Universities (TSSSU), funded by the Innovation and Technology Commission (ITC), serves as the bedrock of Hong Kong’s university-led entrepreneurship. However, a granular comparison between the University of Hong Kong (HKU) and the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST) reveals two fundamentally different execution philosophies. HKU’s implementation, managed through **iDendron** and the **Techno-Entrepreneurship Core (TEC)**, has evolved into a "Founder-First" ecosystem characterized by aggressive revenue-sharing incentives (the 80/20 split). In contrast, HKUST’s framework, overseen by the **Entrepreneurship Center (EC)** and the **Office of Knowledge Transfer (OKT)**, prioritizes "Deep-Tech Pipeline" efficiency, leveraging a low-friction equity model (the 3% stake) to accelerate the commercialization of heavy engineering and research-driven assets.

For Project Phoenix, the choice between these two frameworks is not merely a matter of funding, but a strategic decision regarding intellectual property (IP) control, founder incentives, and the speed of the spin-off approval cycle.

---

#### 2. Funding Mechanics: The HK$1.5M vs. HK$1.3M Internal Delta
While the ITC mandates a maximum annual ceiling of HK$1.5M per start-up under both the TSSSU-O (Original) and TSSSU+ (Enhancement) tracks, the two institutions apply these caps differently.

**HKU (The Max-Cap Strategy):**
HKU typically allows top-tier applicants to apply for and receive the full **HK$1.5 million** annual grant. This strategy is designed to support high-burn R&D projects, particularly in MedTech and BioTech, where initial equipment and clinical trial costs are prohibitive. By offering the maximum ceiling, HKU attracts mature research teams that require significant capital to cross the "Valley of Death." (Source: [hku.hk](https://tto.hku.hk/tsssu))

**HKUST (The Volume-Distribution Strategy):**
HKUST has historically implemented an internal "recommended" or historical cap of **HK$1.3 million**. Because the ITC allocates a fixed pool (currently HK$16M total per university, split $8M for TSSSU-O and $8M for TSSSU+), HKUST’s strategy is to distribute the funds across a larger number of "DeepTech" teams. This ensures that more research-based start-ups receive seed funding, fostering a broader technical ecosystem rather than concentrating funds in a few "super-startups." (Source: [hkust.edu.hk](https://okt.hkust.edu.hk/funding/tsssu))

**Strategic Implication for Project Phoenix:**
Corporate spin-offs requiring maximum initial subsidy should prioritize the HKU framework. Conversely, spin-offs that are part of a larger technical cluster and can benefit from HKUST’s broader technical network may find the $1.3M cap acceptable in exchange for higher ecosystem support.

#### 3. Equity and Ownership Models: The 80/20 vs. 3% Framework
The most critical point of divergence for corporate strategists is the "Price of Participation"—the equity and revenue split mandated by the university.

**HKU: The "80/20" Revenue Split and 20% Membership Rule**
In a radical policy shift in 2022-23, HKU moved to an **80/20 Revenue Split model**. Under this policy, inventors (founders) retain **80% of net licensing revenue**, while the university retains 20%. This is arguably the most generous incentive structure in Asia. 
*   **Ownership Requirement**: To be eligible for TSSSU@HKU, HKU members (staff, students, or alumni) must collectively own **at least 20%** of the start-up.
*   **Strategic Goal**: This model is designed to maximize personal financial incentives for professors and researchers, encouraging them to leave the lab and lead the venture. (Source: [hku.hk](https://tto.hku.hk/about/policy))

**HKUST: The "3% Equity" and 10% Membership Rule**
HKUST utilizes a standardized, low-friction **3% Equity model** for licensing university IP. Instead of high upfront cash royalties, the university takes a small, often non-dilutive equity stake.
*   **Ownership Requirement**: HKUST members must own **at least 10%** of the start-up. 
*   **Strategic Goal**: This reduces the "cash tax" on the start-up during its early years. By taking equity instead of high royalties, HKUST aligns its long-term interests with the company’s exit value rather than its immediate licensing revenue. (Source: [hkust.edu.hk](https://okt.hkust.edu.hk/ip-policy))

**Comparison Table: Equity and Incentives**
| Feature | HKU iDendron | HKUST EC |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **IP Revenue Split** | 80% (Founder) / 20% (Uni) | ~50% (Founder) / 50% (Uni) (Tiered) |
| **Standard Equity Stake** | 5%–10% (Negotiated) | ~3% (Standardized) |
| **Min. Member Ownership** | 20% | 10% |
| **Primary Incentive** | Immediate personal wealth | Long-term company valuation |

#### 4. Licensing Paths: "Express" vs. "Standard"
Both universities have introduced "Express Licensing" to bypass the months-long legal negotiations that often kill university spin-offs.

**HKU Express Licensing:**
HKU’s **Express Licensing** path uses non-negotiable templates for start-ups. It features low upfront fees and royalties typically capped at 1–3%. Its primary innovation is the ability to **defer patent reimbursement costs** until a Series A funding round, preserving critical seed capital for operations. (Source: [tto.hku.hk/express-license])

**HKUST Express Licensing Platform (ELP) & TAP:**
HKUST’s **Technology Adoption Program (TAP)** takes "Express" a step further by offering **upfront free licenses** for a selection of mature patents. Start-ups pay zero to access the tech initially, with "running royalties" only kicking in after commercialization. This "pay-as-you-grow" model is perfectly suited for Project Phoenix spin-offs that have a long development lead time. (Source: [ttc.hkust.edu.hk/express-licensing])

#### 5. Success Rates and Disbursement Timelines
Success in the TSSSU framework is often determined by the university’s internal selection panel.

**Selection & Success Rates:**
*   **HKU**: In the 2023-24 cycle, HKU awarded funding to **32 start-ups**. Their selection focus is interdisciplinary, with high preferences for FinTech, MedTech, and "Social Impact" ventures.
*   **HKUST**: HKUST is more "Hard Tech" focused. While their cohort sizes are similar, the competition is significantly higher for engineering-heavy projects. HKUST explicitly prioritizes applicants that are **commercializing HKUST-owned IP**.

**Disbursement Cycle (Reimbursement vs. Advance):**
*   **HKUST Advantage**: HKUST explicitly allows for an **advance payment of up to 50%** of the approved funding. This is a massive "alumni-led" preference, as it allows founders to hire staff and buy equipment before the reimbursement claims are audited.
*   **HKU Cycle**: HKU operates on a more traditional reimbursement model, requiring rigorous biannual audits (Annex C) before funds are released. (Source: [itf.gov.hk/tsssu-guidelines])

---

#### 6. Alumni-Led Preferences and Spin-off Approval Cycles
Project Phoenix requires a fast "Spin-off" approval cycle.

**HKU: The "DeepTech100" Accelerator**
HKU has introduced the **DeepTech100** program, which acts as a "Green Lane" for TSSSU. If a team is accepted into DeepTech100, they receive expedited IP licensing and direct access to the HK$400M Entrepreneurship Engine Fund. This reduces the spin-off approval cycle from the typical 6–9 months down to approximately **3–4 months**. (Source: [hku.hk/tec/deeptech100])

**HKUST: The "GBA Pipeline"**
HKUST’s preference for alumni is rooted in its **Greater Bay Area (GBA)** presence. For alumni looking to manufacturing in Shenzhen or scale in Foshan, HKUST offers a superior "Fast-Track" into Mainland research institutes. Their approval cycle is efficient but more focused on technical due diligence via the Licensing Committee.
